Abstract

This project explores how neural activity influences decision making in mice during a visual task. The dataset includes multiple experimental sessions where mice were shown visual stimuli and had to make decisions based on what they saw. Their brain activity was recorded during each trial, along with whether their decision was correct or incorrect. The goal of this study is to build a model that predicts the outcome of a trial based on neural activity and stimulus contrast levels. The project is divided into three main parts. First, an exploratory analysis examines patterns in the data, including differences across sessions and individual mice. Second, data integration techniques are used to account for variations in neural activity between sessions. Finally, different machine learning models are tested to find the best method for predicting trial outcomes. The results show that some models perform better than others, with more complex models providing stronger predictions. The most important factors for determining trial success are neural activity and stimulus contrast, suggesting that brain responses play a key role in decision-making. This study highlights how machine learning can be used to analyze neural data and provides insights into how the brain processes information to guide behavior.

Introduction

Understanding how the brain processes information and makes decisions is a key question in neuroscience. One way to study this is by analyzing neural activity during decision-making tasks. In this project, we explore how neural activity, recorded from mice performing a visual decision-making task, relates to their behavioral outcomes. The dataset, originally collected by Steinmetz et al., consists of multiple experimental sessions where mice were shown visual stimuli and had to respond by turning a wheel to indicate what they saw. Their choices were either correct or incorrect, and their brain activity was recorded throughout the task.

The main goal of this study is to build a model that can predict whether a trial will result in success or failure based on neural activity and the contrast levels of the visual stimuli. To achieve this, the project is structured into three main parts:

  • Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA): Examines patterns in trial outcomes and neural spike counts and identifies differences in neural activity across sessions and individual mice.
  • Data Integration: Accounts for variability in neural activity between sessions and ensures patterns are consistently recognized across the dataset.
  • Predictive Modeling: Tests different machine learning models, including logistic regression, mixed-effects models, Random Forest, and XGBoost and determines the most effective method for predicting trial outcomes.

This study provides insights into how neural activity relates to behavior and decision-making. By applying statistical analysis and machine learning techniques, we aim to understand which factors contribute most to successful decisions and whether neural activity can be reliably used to predict outcomes. The findings of this project could have broader applications in neuroscience, particularly in understanding how brain activity drives behavior in response to sensory information.

Part 1: Exploratory Data Analysis

Before building predictive models, we conducted an exploratory data analysis (EDA) to understand patterns in neural activity, trial outcomes, and variations across different sessions and mice. This section examines key trends in neural spike rates, how they relate to trial success, and how these factors change over time. Additionally, we investigate variability across experimental conditions to determine what adjustments may be needed for effective modeling.

One of the first questions we explored was whether neural spike rates differed between successful and failed trials. The scatter plot comparing the average spike rates in success vs. failure trials (Figure 3) reveals a general correlation, meaning neurons tend to fire at similar rates regardless of trial outcome. However, deviations from the diagonal line suggest that in some cases, neural activity is higher in one condition than the other. This observation raises questions about whether subtle differences in firing rates could still be predictive of success, which we investigate further in later modeling.

Figure 3

Neural activity is not constant throughout the experiment—it fluctuates across trials, which may indicate learning effects, fatigue, or other dynamic changes. A line plot of spike rate trends across trials (Figure 4) shows significant variability, with some neurons displaying consistently high firing rates while others decline over time. This trend could suggest neural adaptation, where neurons become less responsive as trials progress.

Figure 4

To further examine neural adaptation, we compared firing rates in early vs. late trials. The bar chart in Figure 5 shows a clear decrease in spike rates over time, reinforcing the idea that neurons exhibit less activity as the session progresses. Notably, this trend occurs in both success and failure trials, suggesting a general adaptation rather than a trial-specific effect.

Figure 5

At the same time, if subjects are improving over time, we would expect to see an increase in success rates. The success rate over trials (Figure 6) confirms this, with an upward trend that indicates learning. However, the trajectory is not perfectly smooth, and some fluctuations suggest that external factors, such as task difficulty or attention levels, may also be influencing performance. While neural adaptation suggests decreasing activity over time, the increase in success rate indicates that subjects are still improving at the task. This means learning is happening despite reduced firing.

While looking at overall trends is useful, differences between experimental sessions and individual subjects must also be considered. Figure 7 shows a boxplot of total spikes per trial across sessions, highlighting that neural activity levels vary significantly from one session to another. Some sessions have consistently higher spike rates, while others are more variable. This suggests that session-level effects could influence trial outcomes, making it necessary to adjust for them in modeling.

Similarly, Figure 8 presents a boxplot of total spikes per trial across mice. The differences in neural activity between subjects reinforce the need to account for individual variability. Some mice exhibit consistently high spike rates, while others fire at lower rates. These differences may reflect variations in behavior, learning ability, or neural responsiveness.

To support these visual observations with statistical evidence, we conducted several quantitative analyses:

  • Neural Adaptation (Linear Regression): Spike rates significantly decrease over trials (p < 0.001).
  • Learning Effect (Logistic Regression): The probability of trial success significantly increases across trials (p < 0.001).
  • Session Differences (ANOVA): Strong and significant differences in spike rates between sessions (p < 0.001, large effect size).
  • Mouse Differences (ANOVA): Significant but smaller differences between mice (p < 0.001, moderate effect size).

These analyses clearly indicate that neural activity, trial success, session variability, and individual differences are important considerations when building predictive models. The insights gained here inform the data integration strategies we use in Part 2, ensuring our models accurately capture the underlying complexity in the data.

Part 2: Data Integration

In Part 1, we found that neural spike rates, trial outcomes, and their variability significantly differ across sessions and mice. Effective predictive modeling requires addressing this variability. In this section, we propose an approach to integrate the data across sessions by extracting common patterns and addressing differences between sessions to enhance prediction performance.

Extracting Shared Patterns Across Sessions:

To integrate data effectively, we first standardized (scaled) key features: spike rates, contrast_left, and contrast_right. Standardization ensures that variations in measurement scales do not disproportionately influence model predictions. By scaling, we found clearer patterns of neural activity and contrasts across sessions, which helps the model generalize better.

Addressing Differences Between Sessions:

Our exploratory analysis highlighted significant differences in total neural spike counts across sessions. Specifically, an Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) revealed strong statistical differences across sessions:

  • ANOVA Result: Significant variation in spike counts across sessions (p-value < 2e-16, effect size η² = 0.72).
  • Pairwise comparisons (Tukey’s HSD) showed highly significant differences between most session pairs, indicating substantial heterogeneity in neural activity across sessions.

Because sessions differed significantly, we employed a mixed-effects logistic regression model to account explicitly for session variability. In this approach, sessions were treated as random effects—allowing each session to have its baseline level of activity, while still extracting common predictive patterns from the entire dataset.

Mixed-Effects Model Results:

  • Fixed-effects estimates: Contrast_left, contrast_right, and total spikes remained significant predictors of trial success.
  • Random effects: Sessions varied considerably (random effect SD = 0.2383), confirming substantial session-specific differences.

When comparing this mixed-effects approach to a simpler logistic regression model without session adjustments, the mixed-effects model notably improved predictive performance, suggesting that session variability significantly impacts the model.

Addressing Differences Between Mice:

We also considered variations between individual mice. ANOVA indicated significant differences in total spikes between mice (p-value < 2.2e-16, effect size η² = 0.09). Pairwise comparisons (Tukey’s test) revealed differences in neural activity, particularly between Forssmann and Lederberg, suggesting mouse-specific adaptations. However, because session-to-session differences had a larger effect size, our primary data integration strategy emphasized session-level adjustments rather than individual mice.

Why Data Integration Matters:

Effectively integrating data by accounting for session-specific and mouse-specific variability allows our model to leverage the broader dataset more efficiently. This enhances the reliability and generalizability of predictions, setting a strong foundation for our predictive modeling in the next section.

Part 3: Predictive Modeling

In this section, we evaluated several machine learning models to determine which method best predicts trial outcomes using neural spike counts and stimulus contrasts. The models tested included logistic regression, mixed-effects logistic regression, Random Forest, and XGBoost.

Logistic Regression Model:

We first applied a simple logistic regression model. The results indicated significant predictors:

  • Contrast_left: marginal significance (p = 0.0565)
  • Contrast_right: not significant (p = 0.4458)
  • Total spikes: highly significant (p < 0.0001)

The model had moderate predictive ability, with an Area Under the Curve (AUC) of approximately 0.55, indicating slightly better than random chance performance.

Mixed-Effects Logistic Regression Model:

Due to significant session-level differences identified in Part 2, we employed a mixed-effects logistic regression model to explicitly account for variability between sessions. This model showed substantial improvement in recognizing session variability:

  • Fixed-effects estimates:
  • Contrast_left_scaled: significant (z = 9.72, p < 2e-16)
  • Contrast_right_scaled: significant negative effect (z = -15.23, p < 2e-16)
  • Total spikes_scaled: highly significant (z = 19.98, p < 2e-16)
  • Random effects: Session-level variability (standard deviation = 0.2383), confirming significant session-to-session variation.

The mixed-effects model achieved an AUC of approximately 0.55, similar to the simple logistic regression, highlighting the complexity of adequately capturing session-level variability.

Random Forest Model:

We then tested a Random Forest model, which showed improved predictive performance:

  • Out-of-Bag (OOB) Error Rate: 30.45%
  • Confusion Matrix: Strong performance for success trials but limited accuracy predicting failures.
  • AUC: Approximately 0.80, demonstrating significantly enhanced predictive capability compared to logistic regression models.

XGBoost Model:

The XGBoost model provided the strongest predictive performance:

  • Training log-loss: Rapid decrease over successive iterations, indicating excellent learning of patterns in the data.
  • AUC: Exceptionally high at approximately 0.95, substantially outperforming all previously tested models.

Model Performance on Test Data:

When evaluating the final XGBoost model on separate test data from Session 1 and Session 18, the model showed noticeably reduced performance:

  • AUC on Test Session 1: approximately 0.61
  • AUC on Test Session 18: approximately 0.55

The ROC curves (Figure X) illustrate the decline in predictive performance when tested on data not seen during model training. Session 18 particularly shows a noticeable decrease in predictive accuracy. This result underscores the challenges in applying learned patterns to new, unseen data and suggests the need for more robust methods of ensuring model generalizability.

Areas for Improvement:

Several strategies could enhance future predictive accuracy:

  • Feature Engineering: Including additional features, such as mean spike rates per neuron or time-dependent variables, could enhance model predictions.
  • Model Tuning: Further fine-tuning of XGBoost parameters (e.g., lower learning rates or deeper trees) might improve generalization.
  • Cross-Validation: Implementing more rigorous cross-validation procedures would provide more reliable estimates of model performance.
  • Addressing Class Imbalance: Applying methods like oversampling or undersampling could balance the dataset and potentially improve prediction accuracy for less frequent outcomes.

Our analysis demonstrates that advanced machine learning techniques like Random Forest and especially XGBoost significantly outperform simpler logistic regression approaches. However, results from test data clearly indicate that improving model generalization and robustness is crucial for enhancing predictive accuracy in future analyses.

Discussion

Our predictive modeling analysis revealed clear differences in performance between the tested machine learning methods. Simpler methods such as logistic regression provided basic insights but were limited in their ability to capture complex patterns within neural spike data. The introduction of mixed-effects logistic regression addressed session-level variability effectively, but did not substantially improve predictive accuracy.

The Random Forest and XGBoost models significantly outperformed logistic regression methods, reflecting their superior ability to manage nonlinear relationships and interactions between variables. In particular, XGBoost achieved the highest predictive accuracy, demonstrating substantial potential for practical applications. However, the notable reduction in performance on the unseen test data highlights critical issues with generalizability and indicates that our models may have overfitted the training data.

Future analyses should focus on robust cross-validation techniques, extensive feature engineering, and better handling of dataset imbalances. These improvements could substantially increase the reliability and applicability of predictive models based on neural spike data.

Conclusion

This project demonstrated the strengths and limitations of various machine learning approaches to predicting trial outcomes using neural data. The advanced modeling techniques, particularly XGBoost, provided superior performance but also revealed important considerations regarding model robustness and generalizability. By addressing these challenges in future research, predictive accuracy and reliability can be significantly enhanced, contributing valuable insights to neuroscience and related fields.
